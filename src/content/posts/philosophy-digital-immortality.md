Somewhere on a server farm in rural Oregon, there is a folder containing the final voicemail my grandmother left before she died. I have not listened to it in three years. Not because I have forgotten it exists, but because I cannot decide what kind of object it is. Is it a memory? A message? A piece of her that is somehow still present?

The question of what it means to persist after death — digitally, informationally, in the minds of others — is one I have been thinking about since I joined LastRite two years ago. This is my attempt to work through it out loud.

## The Old Bargains We Made with Death

For most of human history, the primary mechanism of immortality was memory — the stories people told about the dead, passed orally through generations until they blurred into legend or dissolved into silence. Writing extended this, but not indefinitely: most written records eventually deteriorate, are lost, or are simply never read again.

The photograph changed something. For the first time, a likeness could outlast the people who remembered the face. This felt, to many people who encountered early photography, genuinely uncanny — there are accounts of daguerreotype subjects refusing to be photographed because they believed it would steal their soul. The anxiety was not entirely irrational. A photograph does capture something. It just doesn't tell you what to do with what it captures.

## What Is New Now

What digital technology offers is not just better preservation, but a fundamentally different relationship with preserved information. A voicemail is not static. It can be shared, copied, heard by people who never met the speaker. It can be amplified. It can, in principle, persist indefinitely without degradation.

This creates possibilities that did not exist before: a great-great-grandchild listening to their ancestor's voice. A person who died at thirty leaving behind enough video, text, and audio that their children grow up with a genuine sense of who they were. Families scattered across continents gathering, asynchronously, around a shared record.

It also creates questions we don't yet know how to answer.

## The Problem of Consent

The most difficult question in digital legacy is also the most fundamental: can the dead consent to how they are remembered?

This is not merely theoretical. We have users who have uploaded content created by people who did not know it would be preserved in this way. We have had requests from family members to add material that the deceased explicitly said they did not want shared. We have watched estranged relatives use memorial platforms to insert themselves into narratives they were excluded from in life.

I do not have clean answers here. But I think the question matters, and I think the technology industry has largely avoided asking it.

## What Persistence Costs

There is a grief researcher named Thomas Attig whose work I keep returning to. He writes about grief as a process of "relearning the world" — of figuring out how to live in a reality that has been fundamentally altered by the absence of someone you loved. Part of that process, he argues, involves allowing the relationship with the deceased to transform rather than remain frozen.

Digital preservation can complicate this. If the voicemail is always there, unchanged, demanding nothing, asking for no evolution — does it help or hinder the process of relearning? I have no definitive answer. I suspect it depends enormously on the person, the loss, the relationship, and a hundred other variables that resist generalization.

What I do believe is this: the goal of a platform like LastRite should not be to pause grief or to simulate presence. It should be to give people the materials they need to do the work of remembering on their own terms — to look, and revisit, and step away, and return, as the living have always done with the traces left by the dead.

The voicemail is still there. One day I'll be ready to listen again.
