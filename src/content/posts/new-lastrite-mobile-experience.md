Mobile is where grief lives. It is where people reach for their phones at 3 a.m. looking for something they cannot name. It is where families share news of a loss before they have even processed it themselves. It is where, months later, someone stumbles across a photograph and finds themselves unexpectedly sobbing in a grocery store parking lot.

Knowing this, we spent the better part of last year rebuilding our mobile experience from the ground up — not just to make it faster or prettier, but to make it feel *right*.

## What Was Wrong Before

Our original mobile app was a port of the desktop interface. That sounds like a neutral starting point, but for an application people use during some of the most difficult moments of their lives, neutrality is a failure mode. The old app was efficient in the way a form letter is efficient: it got the job done, but it communicated nothing about who was on the other end.

Buttons were too small. Text was too dense. Uploading a photograph — one of the most common and emotionally significant actions in our product — required navigating four screens. We knew this needed to change.

## The Redesign Principles

We started by talking to people. Not user research in the abstract sense — we spoke with families who had used our product during active grief, and we asked them to show us exactly how they interacted with it. What we observed shaped three core principles for the redesign.

**Reduce friction at moments of vulnerability.** The most important actions — uploading a memory, sharing a Space, adding a tribute — now require no more than two taps from the home screen. We removed every confirmation dialog that wasn't protecting against data loss.

**Design for one-handed use.** Many people use their phones lying down, or while holding something else. All primary actions are reachable from the bottom third of the screen. Navigation is thumb-friendly throughout.

**Respect the emotional weight of the content.** Typography is larger, breathing room is generous, and the interface avoids the performative busyness that characterizes most apps. When you're viewing a loved one's memorial, the experience should feel like stepping into a quiet room, not opening a dashboard.

## Accessibility as a First Principle

We also made a deliberate commitment to accessibility — not as an afterthought, but as a design constraint from the start. The new app achieves a 4.5:1 minimum contrast ratio across all text, supports Dynamic Type on iOS and font scaling on Android, and has been tested with VoiceOver and TalkBack for full screen reader compatibility.

Grief does not discriminate. Neither should the tools designed to help people through it.

## What's New in This Release

The updated app is available now on iOS and Android. Key changes include the redesigned upload flow, the new home screen with quick access to your most recently visited Spaces, offline reading support for all saved content, and a new quiet mode that suppresses all notifications during hours you specify.

We will be shipping follow-on updates throughout the year. As always, your feedback shapes the roadmap.
